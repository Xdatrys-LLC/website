20 Insider Tips for
Better Smartphone Photographs

Techniques that separate a photograph from a snapshot. Written for the travelling photographer, not the professional.

Every one of these tips works with any smartphone. No apps to buy, no accessories needed, no photography experience required. Just practical techniques you can use on your next walk or trip.
1
Clean Your Lens Regularly
Technique
▾
Why It Matters

Your phone lives in your pocket and accumulates fingerprints and oil on the lens constantly. Even a thin film of grease creates soft, milky images with reduced contrast, especially noticeable in bright light or against a light source.

How to Do It

Use the corner of a soft cotton shirt or a microfibre cloth. A circular wipe with gentle pressure takes three seconds. Make it a habit each time you pull out your phone to shoot. Check the lens before any photograph that matters.

Try it now: Photograph any bright light source — a window, a lamp. Then clean the lens and take the same shot. The difference will surprise you.
2
Chase Golden Hour — Morning and Evening
Light
▾
Why It Matters

The 45 to 60 minutes after sunrise and before sunset produce warm, directional, low-angle light that flatters every subject. The same street that looks flat at noon becomes cinematic at 6pm.

The Science

When the sun sits low, light travels through more atmosphere, filtering out blue wavelengths and leaving warm orange-gold. The low angle also creates long shadows that give texture and depth to any flat surface.

How to Use It

Search "sunset time [city]" to plan your shots. Position yourself so the light comes from the side. Sidelight creates texture. Backlight creates glowing silhouettes. Front light (sun behind you) is the least interesting option of the three.

Try it now: Find a textured wall and photograph it at noon. Then photograph the same wall 30 minutes before sunset with the light raking across it. The difference demonstrates everything golden hour does.
3
Tap to Focus and Lock Exposure
Technique
▾
Why It Matters

When you tap the screen to focus, the camera also sets its exposure based on that point. If your subject is in shadow but the background is bright, tapping the background makes your subject go dark. Tap on your subject instead.

The AE/AF Lock

On iPhone: tap and hold on your subject until you see AE/AF LOCK appear. This locks both focus and exposure so they do not shift when you recompose. On Android: tap and hold performs the same function on most camera apps.

The Exposure Slider

After tapping, a sun icon (iPhone) or brightness slider (Android) appears. Drag it down to recover blown highlights in sky, white buildings, or bright water. Slightly underexposing is almost always better than overexposing, as you can recover shadows but not blown highlights.

Try it now: Stand near a bright window. Take one photo letting the phone auto-expose. Then tap on the shadowed part of the room and take another. Compare the two results.
4
Use Grid Lines — Rule of Thirds Every Time
Composition
▾
What It Is

Turn on grid lines in your camera settings (Settings → Camera → Grid on iPhone; similar in Android camera settings). The grid divides the frame into thirds both horizontally and vertically, creating nine zones with four intersection points.

How to Use It

Place your main subject on one of the four intersection points rather than dead-centre. Place the horizon on the upper or lower third line rather than across the middle. Both moves create more visually interesting images than centred compositions.

When to Break It

Centred compositions work well for symmetrical subjects: archways, doorways, reflections, faces looking directly at the camera. The rule of thirds improves asymmetric scenes. When in doubt, take both versions and compare.

Try it now: Enable grid lines and photograph a landscape or street scene. Place the horizon on the upper third line (more foreground), then on the lower third line (more sky). Notice how the emotional weight of the image shifts.
5
Never Pinch the Screen to Zoom
Technique
▾
What Pinching Does

Never pinch the screen. Digital zoom, which is what pinching the screen does, is simply cropping. It discards pixels and reduces image quality. A 5x digital zoom on a 12MP camera gives you a 0.5MP crop. The result looks soft, grainy, and flat.

How to Zoom Correctly

Modern phones have multiple physical lenses with true optical zoom. On iPhone, the fixed buttons (0.5x, 1x, 2x, 3x) are optical. On Samsung and Android phones, the labelled buttons (1x, 3x, 5x, 10x) are optical. Use only these fixed positions. Any value between them is digital zoom and degrades the image.

Move Your Feet First

Before reaching for any zoom, move physically closer to your subject. The 1x lens on any modern flagship captures the most detail. Closing the distance gives you more to work with than any zoom setting can provide.

Try it now: Photograph something 10 metres away at 1x optical. Then photograph it at 5x optical. Then photograph it by pinching to 5x digital. Zoom into all three results and compare the edge sharpness. The optical results are clean. The digital zoom result is not.
6
Use Burst Mode for Action and Unpredictable Moments
Technique
▾
What It Does

Burst mode captures multiple frames per second while you hold the shutter down. From 20 to 30 frames, you choose the single sharpest, best-timed image. This is the fastest way to improve your hit rate on moving subjects.

How to Activate

iPhone: slide the shutter button to the left (press-and-hold triggers video, sliding triggers burst). Samsung and most Android: press-and-hold the shutter button. Most camera apps have a burst or continuous shooting mode in the settings.

Best Uses

Children playing, street photography, sports, waves and water, birds in flight, market scenes with people walking through frame, anyone laughing or animated. Any scene where the perfect moment is unpredictable.

Try it now: Ask someone to wave at you and burst-shoot the wave. Review the 20+ frames and find the one where hand position and expression peak together. That selection skill is what burst mode makes possible.
7
Use Leading Lines to Draw the Eye
Composition
▾
What They Are

Leading lines are any visual element that creates a line from the edge of the frame toward the main subject: roads, paths, fences, rivers, shorelines, corridors, rows of trees, walls, railway tracks, jetties.

How to Use Them

Position the start of the line in a lower corner of the frame and let it guide the eye toward your subject in the middle or far distance. The viewer's eye follows the line without thinking. You are guiding where they look.

The Most Powerful Version

Two parallel lines (a road, a corridor) that appear to converge at a vanishing point create a strong sense of depth. Stand in the centre of the lines and shoot down them. The eye travels all the way to the horizon.

A jetty extending into the sea: stand at the shore end, crouch low, and shoot down the jetty toward the horizon. The planks converge and the eye follows them all the way out to the water.
Try it now: Find a path, road, or corridor. Crouch low and shoot down its length. Notice how much more depth the image has compared to shooting across it.
8
Try Night Mode — But Brace the Phone
Technique
▾
How It Works

Night Mode takes multiple exposures and merges them into one clean result. It effectively gives you a 3 to 5 second exposure without a tripod. The software aligns the frames and averages out the noise to produce a clean image in very low light.

The Critical Rule

Night Mode takes 3 to 10 seconds to complete. Any movement of the camera creates blur. Brace the phone against a wall, lamp post, table, or ledge before shooting. The steadier the phone, the longer the exposure Night Mode uses, and the better the result.

Best Uses

City lights at night, light trails, neon reflections in wet streets, candlelit restaurant scenes, star photography, illuminated buildings at dusk. Night Mode handles colour temperature shifts particularly well.

When Not to Use It

Moving subjects — people walking, cars, flowing water. Night Mode blurs anything that moves during the exposure. For moving subjects at night, turn Night Mode off and accept more noise, or use the flash.

Try it now: At night, press the phone flat against a smooth wall and photograph a lit scene. Then take the same shot handheld. Compare the sharpness of the static elements in each result.
9
Use Portrait Mode at the Right Distance
Technique
▾
The Sweet Spot

Portrait mode works best when your subject is 1 to 2 metres from the lens and the background is at least 2 to 3 metres further behind. Too close and the blur looks artificial. Too far and the depth separation disappears entirely.

Not Just for Portraits

Portrait mode works beautifully on food, flowers, objects on a table, architecture detail, and any close-up subject you want to isolate from a busy background. Try it on a coffee cup in a café. The background blurs, the cup stays sharp, and the image looks considered rather than snapped.

Adjust the Blur Afterwards

After shooting, you can adjust the blur intensity in your photo app. On iPhone in Photos, on Samsung in Gallery. Subtle blur (f/5.6) often looks more natural than maximum blur (f/1.4).

Try it now: Place any small object on a table with a cluttered background. Take one standard shot and one Portrait mode shot from 1 metre away. The background reduction demonstrates exactly what the mode does.
10
Shoot Reflections — Water, Glass, Puddles
Composition
▾
Why They Work

A reflection doubles the visual content of a frame, creates symmetry, and often captures colour and light in a way the subject alone cannot. A puddle in a dull street can contain an extraordinary reflection of sky, architecture, and light.

How to Photograph Them

Crouch as low as possible, ideally getting the phone at water or puddle level. The lower you are, the larger the reflected image appears relative to the frame. Tap the reflection to set focus and exposure on it, not the sky above.

Best Reflective Surfaces

Still water (lakes, harbours, canals at dawn), wet streets and pavements after rain, glass building facades, puddles, polished stone floors. After rain is the single best condition for urban reflection photography.

Try it now: After any rain, find a puddle and photograph what is reflected in it from a crouching position. Rotate the final image 180° and observe how it works as a standalone photograph.
11
Find Frames Within Frames
Composition
▾
What It Means

A frame-within-a-frame uses an element in the scene — an arch, a doorway, two close walls, a window, a gap in foliage, a tunnel — to surround your main subject. This creates depth, context, and a sense that the viewer is discovering something rather than simply looking at it.

How to Find Them

Look for archways above and beside your subject. Doorways with a view through to another space. Tree branches overhead. Two columns or pillars on either side. A gap between buildings revealing a street scene.

The Technique

Stand close to the framing element so it fills the edges of the frame. Focus on the subject in the distance, not the frame. The frame itself should be slightly darker and less sharp than the subject. It becomes a visual border, not the point of interest.

Try it now: Find a doorway with a view through to a garden, courtyard, or street. Stand inside and shoot through the doorway. The dark doorframe around a bright exterior creates a natural and satisfying composition.
12
Use Negative Space to Create Impact
Composition
▾
What It Is

Negative space is the empty area around your subject: a clear sky, a still body of water, a plain wall, an empty road. Filling most of the frame with emptiness creates stronger, calmer, more impactful images than crowding the frame with detail.

How to Use It

Place your subject small in one corner or third of the frame and let the rest of the image breathe. The contrast between the small, detailed subject and the large, simple background creates visual tension. The eye goes to the subject precisely because the surrounding space is quiet.

Best Backgrounds for Negative Space

Clear blue sky, still water, flat sand, snow, plain painted walls, smooth concrete, fog. Any uniform, texture-free surface works. The cleaner the background, the stronger the subject isolation.

Try it now: Place a single object on a plain table or floor. Position it in the lower-right third of the frame and fill the rest of the image with the plain surface. Notice how the emptiness gives the object presence.
13
Shoot Overcast — Clouds Are a Giant Softbox
Light
▾
Why Overcast Is Underrated

An overcast sky acts as a massive diffusion screen. It scatters the sunlight evenly in all directions, removing harsh shadows and reducing contrast. Portrait and food photographers pay thousands for studio lighting that mimics what an overcast sky provides for free.

What It's Best For

Portraits (skin tones are smooth and even with no shadow under the eyes), forest and woodland (green foliage saturates beautifully without blown highlights), street photography (building facades show even detail without harsh shadow bands), markets and street food.

What to Avoid

Leave the sky out of your frame on overcast days. A white or grey sky adds nothing and tends to overexpose. Compose looking down or straight ahead rather than upward. The light is your friend; the sky is not your subject.

Try it now: On a bright sunny day, find a patch of shade and photograph a person's face. Compare with a portrait taken in direct sun. The shade version will almost always be more flattering. Overcast light produces the same quality across the entire outdoors.
14
Change Your Angle — Low, High, Close
Composition
▾
The Problem with Eye Level

Most phone photos are taken from standing eye level — the same angle everyone sees, the same angle all snapshot photographers use. Changing your physical position is the single fastest way to make your photos look different from everyone else's.

Go Low

Crouch, kneel, or put the phone at ground level. Low angles make subjects look more powerful, make reflections appear larger, reveal patterns in floors and pavements, and show the undersides of plants and architecture that nobody ever photographs.

Go High

Stairs, balconies, hillsides, bridges, and upper-floor windows give overhead perspectives that transform familiar scenes. People become graphic elements in a pattern. Streets become abstract compositions of geometry and shadow.

Get Close

Most people stay too far back. Move toward your subject until it fills at least a third of the frame. The intimacy of a close shot creates emotional impact that distance cannot provide.

Try it now: Photograph any ordinary object from three positions: eye level, crouching at table height, and directly overhead. Compare all three. The angle completely changes the character of the image.
15
Use the Volume Button as Your Shutter
Technique
▾
Why It Reduces Blur

Pressing the on-screen shutter requires a tap that can move the phone fractionally at the moment of capture. Using the physical volume button as a shutter gives you a more stable grip and a more natural pressing motion, with less camera shake as a result.

How to Use It

On both iPhone and most Android phones, pressing either volume button fires the shutter in photo mode. Grip the phone with both hands, hold it like a camera with your index finger on the volume-up button, for a much steadier shot.

The Two-Hand Grip

Hold the phone with both hands: one supports the bottom, one holds the sides with the thumb on the volume button. Tuck your elbows against your body. This three-point grip reduces camera shake significantly, especially in low light.

Try it now: In a low-light room, take one photo tapping the screen shutter and one pressing the volume button with a two-handed grip. Zoom in to compare the sharpness of fine detail like text or fabric texture.
16
Set a One-Press Shortcut to Open Your Camera
Technique
▾
Why Speed Matters

The best photographs happen in seconds. By the time you unlock your phone, find the camera app, and open it, the moment is gone. Most people miss the shot that mattered because they were still opening the app. A one-press shortcut eliminates that delay entirely.

iPhone Setup

Go to Settings → Accessibility → Accessibility Shortcut, or use the Action Button on iPhone 15 Pro and later (Settings → Action Button → Camera). On any iPhone, you can also swipe left from the lock screen to open the camera directly without unlocking the phone.

Android Setup

Most Android phones support double-pressing the power button to open the camera. On Samsung: Settings → Advanced Features → Side Button → Double Press → Open Camera. On Google Pixel: Settings → System → Gestures → Quickly Open Camera. Check your phone's settings under gestures or shortcuts.

Why This Changes Everything

With the camera one press away, your instinct to shoot stops fighting your phone's lock screen. You start seeing photographs instead of fumbling for them. The photographer who gets the shot is always the one who was ready.

Try it now: Set up the double-press shortcut on your phone today. Then, for the next hour, try opening your camera with the shortcut every time you notice something worth photographing. Notice how many more moments you actually capture.
17
Use Telephoto Compression for Architecture
Technique
▾
What Compression Does

A telephoto lens (3x or 5x on a modern phone) flattens the apparent distance between objects. A row of buildings that looks spread out at 1x becomes a dense, layered stack of facades at 5x. The same scene looks more dramatic and more graphic from a telephoto distance.

How to Use It for Architecture

Move away from your subject far enough that you need the telephoto to fill the frame. A church that looks mediocre from 50 metres at 1x can look extraordinary from 200 metres at 5x. The compression makes the building look taller, the surrounding buildings closer, and the entire scene more monumental.

Specific Shots It Creates

A street that seems to go on forever. Rooftops layered in depth. A mountain that appears to touch the skyline of a city below. Boats in a harbour stacked against each other. Market awnings compressed into a continuous band of colour.

Try it now: Find a straight street or row of buildings. Photograph it from close with the 1x lens. Then walk 200 metres away and photograph the same view at 5x. Compare the sense of scale and depth in each image.
18
Two Edits Are All You Need — Straighten and Crop
Philosophy + Technique
▾
The Philosophy

Google, Adobe, and every photography app maker wants you to believe that great photographs need extensive post-processing. They do not. Great photographs are made in the camera, not on a screen afterwards. The travelling photographer who learns to see, compose, and shoot well needs exactly two post-capture adjustments: straighten a crooked horizon, and crop out a distraction at the edge of the frame. Nothing else.

The Xdatrys approach: Photograph beautifully in the first place. Spend your time looking, seeing, and composing before you press the shutter. The photograph that needs no fixing is always better than the one that has been rescued.
How to Straighten on iPhone

Open the photo in Photos. Tap Edit. Tap the crop icon at the bottom. The Straighten wheel appears. Rotate it until the horizon sits level. Tap Done. That is the entire workflow. No app to download, no subscription required.

How to Straighten on Android

Open the photo in Google Photos. Tap Edit. Tap Crop. The straighten control appears as a rotation dial below the image. Drag it until the horizon is level. Tap Done. No app, no subscription, no cost.

When to Crop

Crop only to remove a specific distraction at the edge of the frame: a bin at the corner, a stranger's arm, a parked car intruding from below. Crop to remove; not to resize, not to reframe, not to fix a composition that should have been composed correctly in the camera.

Try it now: Find a photograph you took recently with a slightly crooked horizon. Open it in your phone's native photo app and straighten it using only the built-in tools. No download required. Notice how much more settled the image feels with a level horizon.
19
Wait for the Person in the Frame
Technique
▾
Why People Complete a Scene

A great location without a human presence is geography. Add one person in the right position and it becomes a story. The person gives scale, emotional context, and a focal point for the viewer's eye. The position and timing of that person in the frame is everything.

The Technique

Compose your shot without anyone in it. Identify where in the frame a person would complete the composition: typically at an intersection point, in a pool of light, or silhouetted against a bright background. Then wait. People will walk through. Take burst shots until someone passes through your ideal position.

What to Look For

A person mid-stride with both feet off the ground rather than flat-footed. A person carrying something colourful. A person facing away from the camera. A small figure against a large scene that emphasises scale.

A saffron-robed monk walking beneath a temple gate. A single child running through an empty square. One tourist looking up at a building while everyone else walks past. The person who makes the scene complete.
Try it now: Find any interesting architectural or street scene. Compose the frame empty. Enable burst mode and wait for 5 minutes. Review the burst frames and find the one person who, at one specific moment, completed the composition.
20
Review Your Failures — That's How You Improve
Technique
▾
The Improvement Loop

Every photographer improves fastest by studying their own failures. After a day of shooting, scroll through all the images you chose not to keep. Ask: why didn't this work? Was the light wrong? Was the composition weak? Was the timing off? Did I move the camera?

The Questions to Ask

Is the subject in sharp focus? If not, use tap-to-focus or burst mode next time. Is the exposure correct? If not, lock exposure with a long tap before shooting. Is the horizon straight? If not, use grid lines. Is there a clear single subject? If not, move closer or simplify the background.

The One-Image Discipline

Select one single image from each day of travel that best represents that day. Not your favourite: the best photograph on its own terms. Choosing one forces you to develop critical judgment about what makes a photograph work. That judgment is the skill that improves everything else.

Try it now: Open your camera roll from your last trip. For each location visited, select one single image that stands alone as a photograph. Not a record, not a memory: something a stranger would stop and look at. That selection process is the training.
